AI in Education

     The personalized learning with AI refers to the customizing of educational experiences according to the individual needs, speed, and preferences of the learner by artificial intelligence systems. These systems enable real-time adaptation of lessons, activities, and assessments according to student performance, engagement, or learning style. Michael J. Luckin and others (2016) note that the rationale behind this approach is that learning outcomes will be maximized when an effort is made to recognize that each student is a little different.

      Rather than putting students through a predetermined course of the same material within the same timeframe, like what happens with old-fashioned schooling, personalized AI-enabled learning grabs content dynamically itself. Most conventional ways have a one-size-fits-all model and in contrast, AI recognizes data to find what each learner knows and what they need to know next. Thus, it can permit struggling learners to get more on-course practice while providing advanced instruction to those skilled enough, all of which leads toward much more equitable and efficient education (Pane et al., 2015).

      In the 1960s and 70s, personalized learning had taken root early through computer-assisted instruction. It received further impetus with the advent of Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) in the 1980s, and much later in the field of machine learning and big data, starting around the 2000s. The main point is that with increases in computational resources and data analysis capacity, adaptive scalable systems have made their way into classrooms and online platforms all around the world (Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019).

      AI for personalized learning is more relevant today than ever, given the increasing number of diversities in classrooms, learning gaps, and requirements for flexibility and a complete online learning solution. It represents the scalable means to address individual learning needs for the sake of inclusivity and accessibility. Particularly after the disruption caused by COVID-19, a growing number of schools and universities turned to AI tools to keep things going, improve outcomes, and ease the burden on stressed-out teachers (Holmes et al., 2019).

What is the role of AI in Personalized Learning

      AI becomes a tutor and an assistant in personalized learning; it does that by evaluating a student's data to recommend lessons in real-time and adjust lesson plans and give timely and targeted feedback. AI identifies misconceptions, gives hints, and schedules reminders for reviews when a student is likely to forget something. It gives teachers an easier chance of keeping up with progress and intervening when necessary (Luckin et al., 2016).

      Machine learning algorithms, natural language processing (NLP), predictive analytics, and recommender systems are key technologies for AI-enabled personalized learning. By these means, AI interprets input provided by students, predicts their further performances, and dynamically selects learning materials and strategies for each individual student (Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019).

      AI may perform a number of educational tasks that naturally include automated grading, content recommendations, knowledge tracing, progress tracking, and conversational tutoring through chatbots. For instance, it could involve real-time re-sequencing of the curriculum based on mastery, suggesting review materials just before a test, or varying assessment tasks according to the proficiency level of the student (Holmes et al., 2019).

      To teach students more efficiently, AI excels in speed, scale, and data accuracy, whereas teachers do a better job in emotional support, ethical judgment, and motivation. AI analyzes large datasets quickly, instantaneously provides feedback, and customizes lessons for thousands of different students simultaneously. But the best results emerge when AI supplements instruction rather than replaces it. Evidence indicates that teachers supported by AI excel in making informed decisions as well as giving more time to high-impact teaching (Pane et al., 2015).

Real World Application of AI in Personalized Learning

      Knewton, Squirrel AI, Carnegie Learning, Duolingo-pampered personalized learning has become a commercial enterprise. Each of these tools adapts difficulty and content to individual performance. For example, in China, Squirrel AI helps students close learning gaps with adaptive technology, thus raising the level of their scores. In the U.S., there are attempts at improving retention and graduation rates with some form of AI tools at Arizona State University. These real-life implementations demonstrate how AI applied on a large scale can promise enhanced learning outcomes (Holmes et al., 2019; Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019).

      DreamBox is chiefly an AI-based platform for personalized learning for mathematics; Smart Sparrow was developed for adaptive eLearning; and Knewton is applied for the higher education sector. All these tools use machine learning to personalize the very nature of content delivery, depending on student feedback. Duolingo, a popular application for language learning, uses AI to determine the difficulty of the lesson based on the users' progress in the course (Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019).

      Countries paying utmost attention to applications of AI in education include China, the United States, and Singapore. China investing in EdTech startups such as Squirrel AI affirms the national commitment toward it. As for the USA, universities like Arizona State and Stanford are attaching this feature into their courses. Singapore's Ministry of Education has piloted AI tools in the classroom as part of its Smart Nation initiative (Luckin et al., 2016).
