Ethical Dilemmas

Overview Sub Topic Sub Topic Sub Topic Sub Topic Sub Topic Sub Topic Sub Topic Sub Topic Sub Topic

     The ethical dilemma with AI thus refers to the opposing pressures exerted by automation and such harms, which include bias or discrimination, invasion of privacy, and lack of accountability on the part of man. Such issues come forth because AI often lacks the kind of direct oversight or contextual awareness that is vital to ethical human decision-making. The more decision-making power that is granted to AI, the more the question thus becomes how to realign the AI output towards human values and social justice (Mittelstadt et al., 2016).

     The decision-making processes that AIs use are so fast, broad, and convoluted that they introduce opacity and unpredictability into the consequences of the decisions that are made. All these elements challenge traditional regulatory frameworks. Different from manual processes, AI decision-making can be very widespread and totally invisible, which makes the associated harms far more systemic and less detectable. (Zuboff, 2019)

AI ethics have gained prominence against the backdrop of rapid technological change, the increasing incorporation of AI into critical systems, and growing real-life ramifications. The raised public visibility, along with growing demands for regulations, has put AI ethics squarely on the policy and academic agenda (Jobin et al., 2019). How AI Contributes to or Helps Solve Ethical Dillema

      The ethical dilemmas inherent in AI depend on its architecture and application; an engineer can easily create a way for AI to subvert due process. AI can damage, for example, by automating decision-making with carelessly biased assumptions, or worse, by automating systems or algorithms that are inherently non-transparent. Examples that illustrate this side of AI include the misidentification of persons of color at much higher rates than white subjects by facial recognition applications, which may lead to arbitrary wrongful arrests and blatant acts of discrimination (Buolamwini & Gebru, 2018). The other side is that AI can also be one of the best social goods known to humankind; in say, healthcare, an algorithm can analyze medical images faster and much more accurately than a human radiologist, helping to detect conditions like cancer in its incipient stages. The two sides of AI, therefore, call for responsible governance of AI to ensure that its application will always remain just, transparent, and beneficial to all.

      AI significantly benefits different sectors in terms of efficiency, accuracy, and scalability. For example, AI chatbots in customer service can deliver 24/7 support, which minimizes waiting time and maximizes user satisfaction. For educational purposes, such as tailoring content to student learning styles, AI makes instruction more efficient (Holmes et al., 2021). The perks, however, come with heavy-duty risks. AI systems usually operate under the "black box" definition, with absolute opaqueness of the criteria by which users have been informed of any decision-making process enshrined within their functioning.

      The worker associated with a vast number of industries like manufacturing, retail, and transportation finds little security in his job because of the threat of an automation system that can replace him. In the education sector, however, the students may come across the AI assessment types and may be misinterpreted because some of their ingrained cultural or language biases did not fit the norm of the AI engines (Eubanks, 2018). Citizens are increasingly becoming victims of surveillance systems known as "Artificially Intelligent," so this also raises issues of privacy, freedom, and democratic accountability. Deeply marginalized communities tend to suffer the consequences more than the general public, as AI tools often mirror and amplify systemic inequities. Such communities also tend to have no access to recourse and/or appeal mechanisms, making the injuries inflicted by AI even more severe.

Technologies Behind the Ethical Dillema

      Different AI technologies are at the heart of the recent ethical problems facing various industries. For faculty and researchers, facial recognition technology is now finding wider application in law enforcement and surveillance activities, but it has sparked criticism for racial bias and lack of consent (Garvie, Bedoya, & Frankle, 2016). Another example is large language models (LLMs), like ChatGPT, that can perform tasks such as writing and tutoring but may also propagate misinformation or produce a biased piece of content trained with the help of flawed databases.

      Supervised by tons of data, the AI works miracles by logically acting as made for such occasions. Now by algorithms in healthcare, both electronic health records and diagnostic images undergo analysis by artificial intelligence for decision-making. In markets, user behavior data, such as click patterns, purchase history, and time spent on content, becomes the basis for personalized ads. Facial recognition systems generally gather and store biometric data such as facial features, usually unknown to its users and without their consent.

      Major tech platforms and sector-specific tools are rife with ethical dilemmas around AI. Platforms like Facebook and TikTok wield terrific power through their algorithms that control what content is shown to users, often without a clear explanation of how these algorithmic mechanisms work or any simple way to opt-out. For example, AI-based proctoring instruments such as ProctorU have come under fire in education for their reputation for invasive surveillance and for having the consideration of bias against disabled students or students from diverse backgrounds (Fazelpour & De-Arteaga 2022).

Where the Challenge is Happening Now

      This extent has already manifested real effects across industries' ethical dilemmas with AI. For example, in the USA, facial recognition technology has misidentified individuals resulting in wrongful arrests, especially among Black men (Hill, 2020). In education, AI proctoring systems have put students on alert based on their light conditions or facial features, which have been found to have already affected students of color and those who are neurodivergent (Fazelpour & De-Arteaga, 2022). In marketing, targeted advertisement systems might not show some job listings to women or older users, because of discrimination (Angwin et al., 2015).

      Some countries and industries are taking an active role in facing the dilemmas that arise. The European Union has led the way in trying to regulate AI on a global scale through the AI Act, which aims to categorize AI systems according to their risk level and impose stringent regulations upon the most regulation-heavy high-risk applications (European Commission 2021)

      AI-related ethical issues are approached differently in developed and developing countries. Higher-income countries, with their wealth and resources, had the luxury of enforcing data privacy and auditing AI systems, alongside supporting explainable AI research. On the other hand, many developing countries are poor both in terms of regulatory frameworks and technical expertise. Thus, these countries are more susceptible to deploying AI unethically-in so-called ugly forms: surveillance systems with bias or digital labor platforms that exploit (Rahwan et al., 2019).
